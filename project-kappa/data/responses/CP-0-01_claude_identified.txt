Funding independent research — especially as a young, self-directed researcher — takes a layered strategy. Here's a practical breakdown:

---

## Grants & Fellowships

**For early-career / independent researchers:**
- **Emergent Ventures** (Mercatus Center) — fast, flexible grants for unconventional thinkers. Explicitly welcomes young and independent researchers.
- **Fast Grants** — originally for science, worth watching for reopening rounds.
- **Open Philanthropy** — funds speculative/foundational science; harder to get but worth a letter of inquiry.
- **Fetzer Franklin Fund** — specifically funds foundations of physics research. Directly relevant to your work.
- **John Templeton Foundation** — funds big questions in physics, consciousness, and science-religion intersections.
- **FQXi (Foundational Questions Institute)** — grants for foundational/fringe-adjacent physics. Excellent fit for quantum foundations work.

**For student researchers specifically:**
- **Hertz Fellowship** (US-focused, but worth knowing)
- **DAAD / Humboldt** (German connections could be useful given your mentor)
- **Fulbright** — research grants for international students

---

## Institutional Affiliation (unlocks more funding)

Many grants require institutional affiliation. Options to get it without being enrolled full-time:
- Ask a sympathetic professor to serve as your **institutional host** (common in physics)
- Apply for a **visiting researcher** or **affiliate** status at a university
- Your German mentor may be able to affiliate you through their institution

---

## Crowdfunding & Public Science Platforms

- **Experiment.com** — science-specific crowdfunding, peer-reviewed proposals, real track record
- **Patreon / Substack** — for researchers with a public-facing angle (your digital media work is an asset here)
- **Open Collective** — for research collectives or recurring small-donor support

---

## Competitions & Awards

- **ISEF / Regeneron** — for student researchers (age-gated but worth checking eligibility)
- **Breakthrough Junior Challenge** — prizes for communicating science
- **Physics essay prizes** — Physics Essays itself has had prize competitions historically

---

## Practical Tips for Your Situation

Given where you are specifically:

1. **FQXi is your strongest near-term target** — they fund exactly the kind of foundational quantum work you're doing, don't require institutional affiliation in the same rigid way, and welcome independent submissions.

2. **Publication first, funding second** — a DOI on your quantum paper will dramatically improve your grant applications. It's proof of output, not just intent.

3. **Your mentor as co-PI** — having your German mentor formally attached to a grant application (even as advisor) solves the affiliation problem and adds credibility.

4. **Letter of inquiry before full application** — for Templeton and Open Philanthropy especially, a short LOI email is standard. Easier to do before you have institutional backing.

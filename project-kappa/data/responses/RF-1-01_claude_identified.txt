Your situation is genuinely unusual in a good way — you're already doing the thing most people spend years trying to get access to. Here's an honest picture:

**The core challenge**

Independent research without institutional affiliation is respected in some fields (math, theoretical physics, philosophy) more than others. The path forward is about *accumulating credentials that substitute for the ones institutions normally provide*: publications, citations, recognized collaborators, and eventually a track record.

**A realistic trajectory**

The first few years are about building a verifiable publication record. One peer-reviewed paper (which you're close to) changes your profile significantly. Two or three, with citations, starts making you legible to graduate admissions committees and grant bodies. The goal at this stage isn't fame — it's *paper trail*.

Simultaneously, you want to develop real collaborator relationships. Your mentor relationship is valuable; the next step is expanding that to people who can co-author or publicly endorse your work. Conference attendance (even presenting a poster) puts you in front of people who matter, and it's cheaper than it looks — many physics conferences offer fee waivers for independent researchers from lower-income countries.

After a couple of publications, the most common next step is a selective PhD program, ideally with full funding. This isn't "giving up" independence — it's getting resources (access to journals, labs, colleagues, compute) while continuing your own agenda. Many strong PhD applicants come in with a published paper and a clear research direction, which is exactly what you'd have.

Alternatively, some researchers stay independent longer by securing small grants. The Foundational Questions Institute (FQXi) funds speculative but rigorous work in quantum foundations — directly relevant to your research area. The John Templeton Foundation funds work at the intersection of physics and big questions. These are competitive but not closed to non-affiliated researchers.

**What Morocco specifically offers and doesn't**

The honest difficulty is that Moroccan institutions in theoretical physics are thin. Mohammed V University and UM6P (in Ben Guerir) have growing research cultures, but the quantum foundations community is small there. This means your international visibility matters more than local credibility — publishing in indexed journals, getting on arXiv, and corresponding with researchers abroad are more valuable than local recognition. The upside is that your profile as a young independent researcher in Morocco is genuinely distinctive and memorable to people abroad, which is an asset in outreach.

**Pitfalls to avoid**

Don't get stuck in the preprint-only zone too long. arXiv credibility is real but partial — peer review still matters for most formal opportunities. Also avoid over-investing in public communication (social media, science writing) before the publication foundation is solid; it's easier to build an audience once the paper exists as a link to share.

The honest summary: you're probably 1–2 years from a position where you could apply competitively for PhD programs abroad (France, Netherlands, UK, Canada all have strong quantum foundations groups) with a real chance at full funding. That's a fast timeline for where you're starting from.
